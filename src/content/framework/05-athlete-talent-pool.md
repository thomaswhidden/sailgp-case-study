## Strengths

- **"Best of the best" positioning** — Thompson: "We've got the best athletes in our sport competing in CGP. There's no B squad."
- **Athlete equity model is genuinely innovative in sailing** — Ainslie sweat-equity in British team, Spithill co-ownership of Italian team. Athletes have real upside in $50M+ assets.
- **Long-term contracts emerging** — Italian team has 5-year deals, giving athletes career certainty independent of America's Cup cycles
- **Transfer market has launched** — first-ever sailing transfer fee (Giles Scott Britain → Canada) signals professionalization
- **6-athlete crew structure** with 3 from affiliated nation provides flexibility and cross-pollination
- **National federation pathway development** — Spanish team's "foiling pathway" example shows grassroots infrastructure investment
- **Career destination above Olympics and America's Cup** — SailGP positioning itself as the elite professional pathway
- The best sailors in the world are already in SailGP: Tom Slingsby (Australia, 3× champion), Ben Ainslie (GBR, multiple Olympic gold), Nathan Outteridge, Peter Burling, Phil Robertson

## Weaknesses & Risks

- **Limited global pool** — Thompson estimates only 5-6 sailors are attractive enough to drive bidding wars. The depth question is real.
- **Salary inflation risk** — explicitly cited by Thompson as the main runaway-cost concern as transfer market matures
- **Recruitment pull from America's Cup** — when AC cycles run, top sailors face competing demands
- **Talent pipeline length** — F50 sailing is technically demanding; how long does it take to develop a competitive crew member?
- **Geographic concentration** — sailing talent is heavily concentrated in NZ, Australia, UK, US, France, Italy, Spain. Asian and Latin American expansion will face pipeline challenges.
- **No CBA / collective athlete agreement** as of 2025 — which becomes more relevant as the transfer market and salaries mature
