## Q&A Summary

**1. Why does SailGP have a real shot at being the next Formula 1?**

Coutts cited three tailwinds: (1) the "Drive to Survive" effect demonstrating that niche motorsports can achieve mainstream cultural breakthrough via docuseries; (2) the pickleball-style new-sport boom showing audiences are open to unfamiliar properties; (3) SailGP's own audience growth (~24% YoY per-event viewership, reaching 13.6M total viewers). The CBS Spain race drew 1.78M viewers — the most-watched US sailing broadcast since 1992, beating the F1 Brazil Grand Prix that same weekend.

**2. How do F50 catamarans differentiate the product?**

The F50 foils at 60+ mph and races within 200 meters of iconic harbor shorelines. This solves sailing's historic TV problem: it is no longer "white triangles on a blue background." The speed, proximity to shore, and the dramatic visual of a 50-foot catamaran flying above the water create a product that is legible and exciting even to viewers with no sailing background.

**3. Why does the regular-season format matter?**

The America's Cup runs every four years. In that model, sponsors cannot plan multi-season commitments, broadcasters cannot build sustained programming around sailing, and athletes cannot build recognizable careers with consistent media exposure. The annual SailGP season format solves all three. Coutts framed this as a structural fix to the Cup's commercial limitations, not merely a scheduling preference.

**4. How is competitive parity developing?**

Australia won the first three championships on the strength of Tom Slingsby's team. By early 2024, all teams except Germany had won at least one race; only two teams had not won a full event. Coutts explicitly connected competitive parity to commercial viability: teams that never win cannot sustain sponsor interest, which makes the whole league more fragile. Spreading wins is therefore a league-level commercial goal, not just a sporting one.

**5. Who are the breakout personalities?**

Phil Robertson of Canada — described by Coutts as the "Mad Max of SailGP" — was identified as the key personality. Robertson's confrontations with umpires and rival teams generate "gold dust" for content and social media. Coutts implicitly endorsed the value of antagonistic, dramatic personalities as part of the entertainment product.

**6. How important is a "Drive to Survive"-style documentary?**

Coutts described this as critical. SailGP's own in-house YouTube series had generated approximately 2.1 million views. He noted "real interest from major players" in a full docuseries. (This became "Uncharted," launched July 2025 on Apple TV+ and multiple other broadcasters.)

**7. What does the Lasry-led US team purchase signal?**

At the time of the interview (January 2024), the Marc Lasry / Avenue Sports Fund acquisition of the US team was the largest team transaction in league history. The ownership group — which included Issa Rae, Deontay Wilder, and Jozy Altidore — was explicitly described by Coutts as a deliberate strategy: celebrity capital brings media coverage that money cannot buy, and the diversity of the investor group sends a signal about who SailGP is for.

**8. What is the tension between Middle East racing and environmental positioning?**

Asked directly about the apparent contradiction of a "Powered by Nature" league racing in fossil-fuel-producing Gulf states, Coutts said: "I absolutely disagree" with the framing. He positioned the Middle East as an opportunity to add value and demonstrate that sustainability can be a global rather than a Western priority. He did not provide specific emissions data or operational metrics. This was a brief response.

**9. Most important leadership lesson from Larry Ellison?**

Coutts recounted asking Ellison for the secret to business success. After a long answer, Ellison stopped himself: the real key is "not giving up." Coutts uses this as his operating philosophy — that persistence through adversity is more differentiating than any specific strategy.

**10. How did you personally get to the top of sailing?**

"By outworking the opposition." Coutts framed his career as a function of work ethic rather than innate talent. This is consistent with his general competitive philosophy: SailGP is winning by working harder on the commercial and product side than the America's Cup establishment has.
