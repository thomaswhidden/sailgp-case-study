## Why this interview matters

Andrew Thompson is SailGP's Managing Director — the operational executive running day-to-day league business. Unlike Russell Coutts (who tells the visionary narrative), Thompson is unusually candid about specific commercial mechanics and discloses financial numbers that Coutts almost never volunteers publicly. This is the highest-yield single source on SailGP unit economics in the public record as of Q3 2025.

**Specific new disclosures:**
- $5M F50 boat cost (centrally manufactured)
- $10M team cost cap (covers everything: salaries, travel, training, marketing, logistics)
- $8–9M top-team annual revenue
- $50M minimum franchise valuation today (up from cost-covering investments in 2018, then $20M)
- 50% broadcast revenue split to teams
- 1.8M average US highlight viewers on CBS — reportedly highest sailing audience on CBS in 40 years
- 70% of Auckland 2025 spectators had never touched a sailing boat
- First-ever sailing transfer fee disclosed (Giles Scott, Britain → Canada)
- Bet365 has already taken SailGP odds

## 1. Origin story & structural thesis

**Why SailGP exists:** The America's Cup is governed by an 1851 Deed of Gift ruled by the New York Supreme Court. Whoever wins the Cup chooses the next venue, the next boat, and takes the commercial rights. This creates total uncertainty cycle-to-cycle.

When Thompson and team were the rights holders for the America's Cup (2010–2017), broadcasters approached them about long-term deals and sponsors wanted multi-cycle commitments. They had to say no every time — they didn't know who would hold the rights next.

**The insight:** Sailing needed a model that wasn't "stop-start." Larry Ellison and Russell Coutts founded SailGP in 2018 explicitly to solve this — a commercially sustainable league with continuous calendar, central rights holding, and predictable structure.

**Ellison's mandate from day one:** "This needs to be run like a business. This isn't some philanthropic venture." The objective from inception was a commercially sustainable sailing league — never been done before in the sport.

## 2. The boats & technology

- **F50 catamarans, foiling, hard wing sail.** Boats can travel 3x faster than the wind, hitting nearly 60 mph while foiling.
- **Why one-design matters:** Even with identical boats, the gap between top and bottom team in a 15-minute race is ~4 minutes. If teams had different equipment, "you'd know the winner before you started."
- **Boat cost: $5 million each.** Each team gets one, plus a shared spares pool for the tour.
- **Lineage:** F50 is a modification of the AC50 used in the 2017 America's Cup, which was itself a modification of the 2013 AC boats. Constantly evolving — they just introduced a new foiling system for more efficiency, durability, and speed.
- **Manufactured centrally** at SailGP's Southampton facility.

**Open data philosophy** — structurally important: SailGP shares all team data across all teams. Lower-ranked teams can see the settings the top teams use. This was a deliberate design decision to close performance gaps and accelerate the move from "feel-based" sailing to data-driven sailing. Top teams are now diving into post-race data; the traditional "going with feeling" approach is no longer competitive.

**Cost structure:** Teams pay two central costs to SailGP — (1) shared services (logistics, race management, umpires) and (2) capital assets (the boats themselves). Set at start of year; SailGP works within that envelope to deliver boats and innovations.

## 3. Ownership model — from league-owned to privatized

**Original 2018 plan:** Full franchise system from day one with owners contributing to a shared pot. "We had a group of owners in the room... thinking we'll all kind of put money in a pot and come by, we'll all kind of work together."

**What actually happened:** Running by committee was too slow. Ellison didn't operate that way. So Ellison personally absorbed the cost and capitalized the league, with the explicit plan that teams would be sold off to private owners over time once value was built.

**The key strategic decision:** Build the property for ~5 years, then carefully select owners. Not a fire sale.

**Franchise valuation curve:**

| Period | Franchise Value |
|---|---|
| 2018–2019 (early days) | Cost-covering investments only |
| ~Season 2–3 | $20M floor |
| Today (2025) | **$50M minimum** |

**What's driving valuations:**
1. League growth and brand recognition
2. **Scarcity** — only so many teams will ever exist
3. **Commercial model now works** — three or four teams will hit at least break-even this season; previously investors had to fund losses
4. **National team ownership is unique in sport** — owners get the full national-market rights for that country (a true national franchise, with the league retaining only the in-country event rights)

**Why national teams (not arbitrary brands):**
- Provides built-in fan base in each country
- Provides talent pool from each nation's sailing infrastructure
- Owners become motivated to drive PR/media in that country (helps league marketing as a side effect)
- Selection criteria for nations: must have sailors who can compete at this level AND an established sailing fan base

## 4. What makes an ideal owner

Thompson's screening criteria for new owners:

1. **Passion for sailing** — must have authentic interest in the sport
2. **Business plan** — can take the franchise and turn it into something valuable, which becomes a model other owners can emulate
3. **Financial backing** — table stakes
4. **Long-term horizon** — explicitly NOT PE that wants to flip in 1–2 seasons

PE interest is significant and growing. SailGP is being selective — wants long-term partners, not short-cycle financial owners.

## 5. Celebrity ownership — the Mbappé deal explained

This is the cleanest articulation of SailGP's celebrity-investor philosophy in the public record.

**The principle:** "We're not just like handing Killian some equity and you do some Instagram posts and thanks very much." Investments must be authentic — the celebrity has to actually care about returns and team performance.

**How the Mbappé deal happened:**
- Sebastian Bazin (CEO of Accor) was already passionate about sailing, sponsoring the French team via Accor
- Accor was also the PSG shirt sponsor and had a personal deal with Mbappé
- The conversation moved naturally from sponsorship to ownership through this existing relationship
- Mbappé's team operates almost like a venture arm — explicitly thinking "what's after football for Killian"
- France is a particularly strong fit because offshore sailing is huge in France

**Thompson does NOT have Mbappé's personal number** — most engagement is through Mbappé's team. This frames how celebrity-owner relationships actually work operationally.

## 6. The financial model — critical numbers

### Cost cap structure
- **$10M USD team cost cap** — covers everything: sailor salaries, accommodation, travel, training, business support, commercial, marketing, boats, logistics
- **Currently in discussion** to *exclude* marketing/social spend from the cap — they want teams to spend more on these areas

### Why a cost cap exists
- Without it, design and build of boats would consume team budgets (America's Cup teams spend ~£150M per cycle on this) — but SailGP has solved this through central design/build
- The next runaway-cost risk is athlete salaries — Thompson estimates 5–6 sailors are now attractive enough to drive bidding wars
- "We don't want teams to create mega teams and then go out of business by doing so."

### Team revenue
- **Top teams generate $8–9M USD per season**
- Primary revenue source: **sponsorship** (boat branding space — "a pretty significant floating billboard")
- League retains some boat branding for Rolex (title sponsor); rest goes to teams
- **Broadcast distribution: 50% of league broadcast rights to teams** (though broadcast revenue is still small overall)
- Other team revenue: hospitality, merchandise, in-territory event partnership

### Athlete equity model
- Athletes can receive equity in teams as part of their package
- **Ben Ainslie example:** Got equity in the British team for bringing in an investor and serving as CEO ("sweat equity"). He now has equity in a $50M+ asset.
- **Jimmy Spithill:** Co-owner and CEO of the Italian team — "kind of payback for all that hard work" with Larry and Russell over the years
- This model is "certainly new in the world of sailing"

## 7. Transfer market — just getting started

- **Six athletes per boat; three must be from the affiliated nation** — dropped from one to three over the past ~year, which opened the transfer market
- **First-ever sailing transfer fee:** Giles Scott from Britain to Canada (Ainslie's team to Canada). Scott is now part of the Canadian ownership group.
- **Transfer fees count toward the $10M team cost cap**
- **Long-term contracts emerging:** Italian team has signed 5-year deals — gives athletes career certainty independent of America's Cup cycles
- **Salary range:** Thompson diplomatically refused specifics, said "somewhere in the middle" of low-hundreds-of-thousands to high millions. Acknowledged America's Cup salaries for the very best are "all bets off."

## 8. League expansion — the 20-team target

**Current state:** 12 teams. Italy and Brazil added for the current season.

**Why Brazil:** Third-rated media market and didn't even have a team. No-brainer despite the work involved.

**Strategic principle:** Adding a team = adding an engaged market that benefits all teams. TV numbers in Italy and Brazil are now meaningfully better.

**Expansion targets named explicitly:** China, Japan, Mexico, more South American markets.

**The cap thinking:** ~20 teams is "probably the right number" — comparison to Premier League (20) and NFL (32). Likely structure: two pools of 10, with a Group A / Group B format.

**Why the format change matters commercially:**
Currently 4 races in 1.5 hours of broadcast, with ~10-minute breaks where viewership drops sharply. With two fleets running concurrently, races run back-to-back ("bang bang bang") — eliminating viewer drop-off.

**Growth pace:** Deliberately one or two teams or one venue per year. Not 8 franchises to highest bidder. May settle at 14 or 16 if that's the right number — they evaluate as they progress.

## 9. Broadcast strategy

### Two five-year arcs
- **First 5 years:** Build a product, build a fan base, get eyeballs
- **Next 5 years:** Convert that audience into commercial broadcast revenue
- Broadcast deals are essentially binary: if SailGP drives subscribers to (e.g.) Canal Plus in France, the broadcaster will pay rights fees. That conversion hasn't happened yet at scale.

### Current broadcast partners
- **UK:** TNT (new partner, helping with promotion)
- **US:** CBS Sports (live racing) + CBS national network (highlights). **1.8M average highlight viewers — reportedly highest sailing audience on CBS in 40 years.**
- **Canada:** TSN ("huge at the moment")
- **New Zealand:** Discovery (free-to-air)
- **Australia:** Fox Sports

## 10. Audience composition

### The killer stat
**Auckland event January 2025: 20,000 paying spectators, 70% had never touched a sailing boat.** SailGP is recruiting non-sailors, not converting sailors.

### Target persona
"Racing fans" — F1 sits at the top of that category, SailGP positioning itself in the second tier of racing properties.

### Demographic split
- Classic 45–55 affluent audience (traditional sailing demographic)
- New TikTok/YouTube audience drawn to extreme sports, crashes, speed
- One viral TikTok clip (NZ team member dangling off the boat) hit ~50 million views
- Behavioral observation: viewers no longer watch full matches across most sports — short-form clips drive fan acquisition

## 11. Athlete profiles & "Drive to Survive"

**Thompson's honest take:** "If I had a pound for every marketing person who said 'storylines, athletes as heroes,' I'd be a very rich man."

**The challenge:** Sailing athletes aren't household names yet. Ben Ainslie is in the UK; Pete Burling is mobbed in Auckland but unknown elsewhere.

**The differentiator:** "We've got the best athletes in our sport competing in CGP. There's no B squad."

**On a Drive to Survive-style series:**
- Confirmed they have a series coming out this year with a distribution partner — this became "Uncharted," released July 2025 (after this interview)
- Existing series: **"Racing on the Edge"** — funded with Rolex, distributed via broadcast partners and YouTube
- Thompson is skeptical of just copying Drive to Survive: "the market is moving on a little bit from that now... what's the next thing for content?"
- Teased "a couple more ideas up our sleeve" beyond the docuseries
- "I'm not saying we've got the magic formula, but it's important to start down that road"

## 12. Positioning in the wider sailing world

**Relationship with World Sailing:** Strong. SailGP is a "special event of World Sailing" with their endorsement.

**Olympic sailing's challenge:** Hard for casual fans to understand. Thompson explicitly asked the host if he watched Paris 2024 sailing — host hadn't. "It's just really hard for the casual sports fan to engage with."

**The fragmentation problem in sailing:**
- Many disciplines, classes, formats
- World Sailing has a complex sport to govern (compared to, say, table tennis where the world championship is just two people playing)

**SailGP's positioning:** "The simple version" of sailing, sitting at the elite level.

**Pathway development:**
- Each team works closely with national federations
- Spanish team created a "foiling pathway" supported by the Spanish federation
- Vision: Olympic pathway → SailGP pathway as the elite professional career destination

## 13. Logistics — the unsung operation

A "huge operation" managed centrally:
- F50 boats are 50ft, but the bow detaches making them 40ft — fits a standard 40ft shipping container
- "Like a Meccano set" — disassembled within 24 hours of a race
- ~140 40ft containers per tour
- **DP World** is logistics partner
- SailGP **chartered its own ship** this year due to challenging shipping industry conditions ("de-risked ourselves")
- Centralization enables economies of scale — 12 teams doing this independently would be far more expensive

## 14. Quick-fire round

**One owner Thompson would bring in:** **Ryan Reynolds.** Praised the *Welcome to Wrexham* model — entertainment product around a sport, not a sports show. *(Note: Reynolds and Hugh Jackman became controlling owners of the Australia team in June 2025, after this interview was recorded — Thompson called this prophetically.)*

**Untapped revenue streams:**
1. **Ticketing** — has grown faster than expected
   - Portsmouth grandstand: £60
   - Premium hospitality (FNB included, "Apres-sail" vibe): £300–400
   - Sponsor areas above premium
2. **Gaming/betting** — SailGP commissioned studies showing the sport lends itself well to betting because anyone can win (one-design = level playing field, like a horse racing handicap). They have an internal team generating odds; **Bet365 has already taken SailGP odds**.

**Hardest thing to navigate at the start:** "We've built nothing and nobody knows who we are." Going from rights holders of the well-known America's Cup to pitching a brand-new property with blank looks at every meeting.

**Biggest learning from Larry Ellison:** "Commercial commercial commercial." Drive value in everything. **No committees**. Decisions made quickly. "It's a no environment" — Russell can say no to Larry and vice versa, and it works because they're both commercially minded.
