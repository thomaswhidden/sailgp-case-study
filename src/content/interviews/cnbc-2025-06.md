## Q&A Summary

**1. What gives you confidence saying SailGP has a "bright future" right now?**

June 2025 was a significant moment: Season 4 had demonstrated 12 teams and 12 events were operationally viable, the Ryan Reynolds and Hugh Jackman announcement for the Australia team was breaking at the same time, and the "Uncharted" docuseries was in post-production ahead of its July 2025 global launch. Coutts has framed this period as the transition from "proving the concept" to "executing the scale-up." The four consecutive years of double-digit audience growth, combined with team valuations rising from $35M to $60M+ in two years, provided the commercial data points to back the confidence.

**2. How does SailGP's revenue mix look, and where does growth come from next?**

The revenue profile heading into Season 5 was still heavily weighted toward host fees (cities paying to host Grand Prix events) and global sponsorship (Rolex as title partner, Emirates as global airline partner from November 2024, DP World, KPMG, Accor). Broadcast rights fees remain relatively modest compared to F1 or the NFL — the CBS deal in the US is a presence deal more than a rights-fee deal. Coutts has consistently identified broadcast and licensing as the two most underdeveloped revenue streams relative to the league's audience size and growth rate.

**3. For investors watching Squawk Box — what is the thesis for buying a SailGP team right now?**

The pitch has three components. First, entry prices are still early-stage: teams in the $35-60M range are priced at a fraction of comparable sports franchise valuations (F1 teams trade at $1B+, NBA franchises at $4B+). Second, the asset appreciates as the league grows: the revenue-to-valuation multiple compresses as SailGP revenue scales. Third, the team ownership model gives investors a scarce, irreplaceable asset — there will only ever be 20 teams by design, and the league controls supply. For celebrity investors specifically, there is also the media and brand amplification effect: the press attention from owning a SailGP team is worth millions in earned media.

**4. Ryan Reynolds and Hugh Jackman just announced they're taking controlling ownership of the Australia team. How significant is that?**

Coutts has described the Reynolds-Jackman acquisition as transformational for the league's brand trajectory in North America and globally. Reynolds in particular brings a proven track record with sports content creation through the Wrexham story. Australia is the right team for this profile: three-time champions, physically spectacular races, Tom Slingsby as a world-class athlete with a compelling competitive narrative. The combination of on-water excellence and off-water storytelling ability is exactly the formula Coutts has been engineering since the league's founding.

**5. Emirates signed a 5-year global airline partnership in November 2024. What does that kind of long-term deal signal?**

The Emirates deal is structurally important because it anchors multi-year commercial planning in a way that one-season sponsorships cannot. Coutts has argued that SailGP's regular-season annual format (versus the America's Cup four-year cycle) specifically enables partners like Emirates to plan sustained activation budgets — they can build marketing campaigns, hospitality programs, and co-branded content series that compound over years. A five-year deal from an airline with Emirates' scale and premium positioning also validates SailGP's target demographic: high-net-worth, internationally mobile, sports-interested consumers.

**6. Season 5 has 12 events across how many continents — can you walk through the expansion?**

Season 5 (2024-25) brought the league to 12 events, with two new venue additions: Italy and Brazil. Brazil represents the first South American event in league history, accompanied by the launch of the Mubadala Brazil team — the first South American franchise. The geographic expansion is not just commercial diversification; it tests the league's operational infrastructure at scale and creates local ownership and fan bases in new markets. Coutts has been explicit that the path to 20 events (roughly F1 scale) requires proving the per-event economics across diverse venue types and geographies.

**7. What is the plan for markets like China, India, Japan, and Korea?**

These are the four markets Coutts has consistently named as the highest-priority next entries. The strategic logic: each is a massive sailing-adjacent market where the F50 product can create a new audience from scratch (unlike established markets where SailGP competes for existing sports attention). The challenge is local ownership — league policy requires team owners with meaningful local market connections. Finding the right ownership group in China or India with the right profile (local credibility, global capital, celebrity adjacency) has been the gating factor.
